# City Portrait Methodology

## Overview

The **City Portrait** is the methodology developed by DEAL (Doughnut Economics Action Lab) in collaboration with Biomimicry 3.8, C40 Cities, and Circle Economy for downscaling the global Doughnut to a city or regional level.

First piloted in 2019 with Philadelphia, Portland, and Amsterdam, the Amsterdam City Doughnut (published April 2020) was the first public City Portrait. Amsterdam subsequently adopted the Doughnut as its official vision for city development.

## The Four Lenses

The City Portrait uses four "lenses" that arise from combining two domains (social and ecological) with two scales (local and global):

### 1. Local-Social
**"What would it mean for the people of [city] to thrive?"**

Examines how residents' needs are being met across the 12 social foundation dimensions. Uses local indicators — health outcomes, education attainment, housing affordability, income levels, food security, etc.

Data sources: Census data, local health surveys, school district data, housing reports, community assessments.

### 2. Local-Ecological
**"What would it mean for [city] to thrive within its natural habitat?"**

Examines the city's relationship with its local ecosystem. Looks at local biodiversity, water systems, soil health, green spaces, local air quality, and ecosystem services.

Data sources: Local environmental monitoring, parks/open space data, water quality reports, biodiversity surveys, urban tree canopy assessments.

### 3. Global-Social
**"What would it mean for [city] to respect the wellbeing of people worldwide?"**

Examines the city's social impacts on people beyond its borders. Considers supply chain labor conditions, trade impacts, embedded labor in imports, corporate practices of major employers.

Data sources: Supply chain audits, fair trade certifications, corporate social responsibility reports, trade data.

### 4. Global-Ecological
**"What would it mean for [city] to respect the health of the whole planet?"**

Examines the city's ecological footprint beyond its borders. Considers consumption-based emissions, embedded water/land/materials in imports, waste exports, and contributions to global ecological overshoot.

Data sources: Consumption-based emissions inventories, ecological footprint calculations, material flow analysis, waste export data.

## Step-by-Step Process

### Phase 1: Initiation & Scoping
1. **Assemble a core team** — cross-sector group including government, academia, community organizations, business
2. **Define geographic boundary** — city limits, metropolitan area, or bioregion
3. **Identify stakeholders** — who needs to be involved, who is affected
4. **Set the intention** — is this for policy, community engagement, research, or all three?

### Phase 2: Data Collection & Portrait Creation
1. **Select indicators** for each of the 12 social dimensions and 9 ecological boundaries
2. **Apply the four lenses** — gather data for local-social, local-ecological, global-social, global-ecological
3. **Set targets/thresholds** — what constitutes "thriving" vs "shortfall" vs "overshoot"?
4. **Collect baseline data** from existing sources (census, environmental monitoring, health surveys, etc.)
5. **Identify data gaps** — what can't be measured yet?

### Phase 3: Assessment & Visualization
1. **Score each dimension** — thriving, shortfall, or overshoot (with gradations)
2. **Create the visual portrait** — the doughnut diagram showing all dimensions
3. **Identify interconnections** — how do dimensions relate to each other?
4. **Produce narrative context** — numbers need stories

### Phase 4: Action & Iteration
1. **Share with community** — public presentation and feedback
2. **Identify leverage points** — where can interventions have the most impact?
3. **Develop action plans** — specific strategies for shortfalls and overshoots
4. **Iterate** — repeat assessment periodically to track progress

## Indicator Selection Principles

- **Locally meaningful** — indicators that resonate with residents
- **Data available** — can be measured with existing or obtainable data
- **Equity-disaggregated** — can be broken down by race, income, gender, geography
- **Actionable** — connected to things local policy can influence
- **Comparable** — allows benchmarking against other cities, state, national averages
- **Target-linked** — has a clear threshold for "thriving"

## Scoring: Shortfall, Threshold, Overshoot

Each dimension gets assessed against a target:

- **Shortfall** (inner ring breach): People's needs are not being met. Falling below the social foundation.
- **Safe/Just** (in the doughnut): Needs are met within ecological limits. The goal state.
- **Overshoot** (outer ring breach): Ecological boundaries are being exceeded.

Gradations are often used:
- Severe shortfall / Moderate shortfall / Near threshold / At threshold / Moderate overshoot / Severe overshoot

## Downscaling Challenges

### For Social Dimensions
- **Data granularity** — city-level data may not exist; county or metro area may be the best available
- **Equity disaggregation** — averages hide disparities; need breakdowns by neighborhood, race, income
- **Target setting** — what "thriving" means can vary by community context

### For Ecological Dimensions
- **Attribution** — how to attribute global impacts to a specific city
- **Consumption vs production** — city may produce little pollution but consume heavily
- **Scale mismatch** — planetary boundaries were defined at global scale; downscaling requires methodological choices
- **Data availability** — many environmental metrics only available at county, state, or national level

## Case Study: Amsterdam City Doughnut (2020)

Amsterdam's portrait was structured around the four lenses:

**Local-Social findings included:**
- 17% of residents experience loneliness
- Growing housing affordability crisis
- Educational achievement gaps by background

**Local-Ecological findings included:**
- Declining biodiversity in urban areas
- Heat island effects
- Water management challenges

**Global-Social findings included:**
- Supply chain labor concerns in garment and electronics imports
- Cacao and palm oil sourcing issues

**Global-Ecological findings included:**
- Large consumption-based carbon footprint
- Embedded land and water in food imports
- Material extraction impacts from imported goods

The portrait was adopted by Amsterdam city government in April 2020 as a framework for post-COVID recovery planning.
